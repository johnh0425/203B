\section{Introduction}
\subsection{Motivation}
Modern VLSI digital circuits demand increasingly stringent performance
and energy efficiency requirements, particularly as transistor scaling continues
to push designs toward higher density and faster clock speeds. A fundamental
component in high-speed digital design is the use of inverter chains (buffer chains)
to drive large capacitive loads, where improper sizing or staging can lead to excessive
delay or unnecessary power consumption. While classical design heuristics such as fixed
stage effort or logical effort methods provide useful guidelines, they do not always yield
globally optimal solutions under complex constraints involving wire parasitics, transistor
sizing limits, and timing requirements. The goal of this work is to develop a principled
optimization framework for inverter chain design that minimizes dynamic power consumption
subject to an Elmore delay constraint. We approach this problem through the lens of convex
optimization by reformulating the inherently non-convex delay expressions into a geometric
programming (GP) framework via logarithmic transformation. This enables efficient computation
of globally optimal inverter sizes and interconnect lengths while accounting for physical
constraints. Through this formulation, we aim to bridge classical circuit design intuition
with modern optimization techniques and provide a scalable method for a energy-efficient
buffer chain design.
\subsection{Previous Works}
	\subsubsection {Low-Power Circuit Design and Delay Modeling}
		The demand for high-throughput, low-power digital systems has become increasingly critical, largely 
		driven by the stringent energy requirements of portable computing applications \cite{cmos}. Power 
		dissipation in digital CMOS circuits primarily stems from switching (dynamic) power, direct-path 
		short-circuit currents, and subthreshold leakage \cite{cmos}. To successfully optimize systems for 
		power, designers rely on mathematical models to predict circuit delay and performance. While 
		transistor behavior in the sub-micrometer regime has been characterized by models such as 
		the alpha-power law \cite{alpha}, designers frequently abstract interconnect and gate delays 
		using resistor-capacitor (RC) equivalent circuits and the Elmore delay approximation \cite{gp}.
		The Elmore delay provides a reliable measure of signal propagation by calculating the area under
		the voltage response curve as an RC network discharges, effectively capturing the time it takes 
		for a signal to transition \cite{gp}.

	\subsubsection{Classical Sizing Heuristics and Iterative Algorithms}
		Historically, optimizing the size of logic gates and buffers to minimize delay or power has been 
		approached through classical design heuristics. A prominent example is the method of "logical 
		effort," which simplifies the delay optimization of logic paths and inverter chains into 
		straightforward sizing rules based on RC models \cite{gp}. While logical effort is an 
		excellent tool for quickly evaluating candidate circuit topologies and gaining early design 
		intuition, it does not always yield globally optimal solutions under complex constraints 
		involving wire parasitics and strict timing requirements. Other modern optimization 
		approaches tackle simultaneous gate sizing, dual-supply voltage ($V_{dd}$), and dual-threshold 
		voltage ($V_{th}$) assignments using sensitivity-based heuristics \cite{dual_vdd}. However, 
		these gradient-descent or heuristic algorithms can be subject to getting trapped in local 
		minima and may struggle to efficiently guarantee a globally optimum power layout \cite{dual_vdd}. 
	\subsubsection{Convex Optimization and Geometric Programming in VLSI}
		To overcome the limitations of heuristic methods, researchers have increasingly turned to convex 
		optimization. The defining advantage of convex optimization is that any local optimum is 
		mathematically guaranteed to be a global optimum, ensuring that solvers do not get trapped in 
		sub-optimal design states \cite{convex}. Furthermore, a specialized subset of convex optimization 
		known as Geometric Programming (GP) has emerged as a unifying standard form for circuit sizing 
		problems \cite{gp}.
	
	The application of GP to digital circuit sizing dates back to 1985 with Fishburn and Dunlop’s TILOS algorithm, 
	which optimized transistor sizing using Elmore delay models \cite{gp}. Today, highly efficient 
	interior-point algorithms can solve these large-scale GP problems reliably and globally, making it possible to 
	account for complex interactions between area, delay, and power constraints.

	Our work builds upon this rich history of geometric programming by applying it specifically to the power-optimal 
	design of inverter (buffer) chains. Instead of relying on traditional iterative heuristics or restricting sizing 
	to gates alone, we treat both the transistor sizing factors ($W_i$) and the physical interconnect 
	lengths ($l_i$) between stages as optimization variables.

	By analyzing the non-convex multiplicative and ratio terms inherent in full RC Elmore delay expressions, 
	we demonstrate that the delay constraint can be reformulated as a posynomial and mapped into a strictly 
	convex geometric programming space using logarithmic variable substitution. Ultimately, our approach 
	bridges the gap between classical circuit design intuition and modern convex optimization techniques. 
	By comparing our method to a simplified Boyd-style baseline that removes wire resistance and 
	capacitance coupling terms, we verify that utilizing a fully wire-aware GP model prevents designers 
	from underestimating the true power costs required to meet strict timing targets.


\subsection{Intended Contributions}
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Optimization-Based Formulation: We formulate the inverter chain design problem as a
	      power minimization problem with Elmore delay, sizing, and interconnect constraints.
	\item Geometric Programming Reformulation: We show that the non-convex delay constraint can
	      be expressed as a posynomial and transformed into a convex constraint via logarithmic variable
	      substitution.
	\item Convexity Verification: We provide a formal justification that the transformed delay
	      constraint is convex by leveraging the log-sum-exp structure and verifying all terms satisfy
	      geometric programming requirements.
	\item Solver Implementation: We implement the resulting formulation in a convex optimization
	      framework (e.g., CVXPY) to compute optimal inverter sizing and interconnect parameters.
	\item Design Insight: We analyze the trade-offs between delay, sizing, and power, and compare
	      the optimized solutions to classical inverter chain design heuristics.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Statement of the Problem}
\subsection{Primal Formulation}
\subsubsection{Physical Interpretation of the Inverter Chain}
We consider a chain of $N$ CMOS inverters used to drive a fixed load capacitance $C_L$.
Such inverter chains, commonly referred to as buffer chains, are fundamental building
blocks in high-speed digital circuits, where they are used to drive large capacitive
loads while meeting strict timing constraints.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{simulation/plots/cmos.png}
    \caption{CMOS Inverter Chain}
    \label{fig:cmos_chain}
\end{figure}

Each stage in the inverter chain is characterized by two primary design variables:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $W_i > 0$: the effective transistor width (or sizing factor) of inverter $i$
	\item $l_i > 0$: the interconnect length between inverter $i$ and inverter $i+1$
\end{itemize}
The objective is to determine the optimal values of $\{W_i\}_{i=1}^N$ and $\{l_i\}_{i=1}^N$
that minimize dynamic power consumption while satisfying a delay constraint.
Each inverter contributes capacitance from two sources:
\paragraph{Gate (Input) Capacitance}
The input capacitance of inverter $i$ is proportional to its size:
\[ C_{in,i} = W_i C_{g0} \]
where $C_{g0}$ represents the gate capacitance of a unit-sized inverter.

\paragraph{Parasitic Capacitance}
Each inverter also has intrinsic parasitic capacitance due to diffusion regions:
\[ C_{p,i} = W_i C_{p0} \]
where $C_{p0}$ is the parasitic capacitance of a unit inverter.

These capacitances contribute to both dynamic power consumption and delay.
The wires connecting inverter stages introduce additional parasitics:
\paragraph{Interconnect Modeling}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Resistance: \[ R_{wire,i} = r\,l_i \]
	\item Capacitance: \[ C_{wire,i} = c\,l_i \]
\end{itemize}
where $r$ and $c$ denote per-unit-length resistance and capacitance, respectively.
These parameters capture the physical effects of interconnect scaling and play a
critical role in delay modeling.

\paragraph{Inverter Resistance}
The effective output resistance of inverter $i$ is inversely proportional
to its size: \[ R_i = \frac{R_0}{W_i} \] where $R_0$ is the resistance of a unit
inverter. Increasing the transistor width reduces resistance, improving delay at the
expense of increased capacitance.

\paragraph{Dynamic Power Objective}
The dynamic power consumption of a digital circuit is given by:
\[ P_{dyn} = \alpha f V_{DD}^2 C_{tot} \]
where $\alpha$ is the switching activity factor, $f$ is the clock frequency, and $V_{DD}$ is the supply voltage.
These quantities are treated as constants in our formulation.

Thus, minimizing dynamic power reduces to minimizing the total switched capacitance $C_{tot}$.

The total capacitance in the inverter chain consists of:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Parasitic capacitance of each inverter
	\item Gate capacitance of downstream inverters
	\item Interconnect capacitance
	\item Final load capacitance $C_L$
\end{itemize}

This yields:
\[ C_{tot} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} W_i C_{p0} + \sum_{i=1}^{N-1} W_{i+1} C_{g0} + \sum_{i=1}^{N} c\,l_i + C_L \]
Since $C_L$ is constant, it does not affect the optimization and may be omitted from the objective.

\paragraph{Elmore Delay Constraint}

To ensure correct circuit operation, the total propagation delay must not exceed a specified clock period $T_{clk}$.
We model delay using the Elmore delay approximation, which expresses delay as a weighted sum of downstream capacitances.
The delay contribution of each stage includes interactions between inverter resistance and downstream capacitances,
as well as interconnect effects.

For stages $i = 1, \dots, N-1$, the delay contribution is:
\[ d_i = \; R_0 C_{g0}\frac{W_{i+1}}{W_i} + R_0 C_{p0} + R_0 c\frac{l_i}{W_i} \\
	+ r C_{g0} l_i W_{i+1} + r C_{p0} l_i W_i + r c l_i^2 \]

The final stage differs since it drives the external load $C_L$:
\[ d_N = \; R_0 C_L\frac{1}{W_N} + R_0 C_{p0} + R_0 c\frac{l_N}{W_N} \\
	+ r C_L l_N + r C_{p0} l_N W_N + r c l_N^2 \]
The total delay constraint is therefore: \[ \sum_{i=1}^{N-1} d_i + d_N \le T_{clk} \]

\paragraph{Design Constraints}
To ensure physically realizable designs, we impose:
\[ W_{min} \le W_i \le W_{max}, \quad W_i > 0, \quad l_i > 0 \]

\subsubsection{Primal Statement}
\paragraph{Geometric Programming Reformulation}
The original formulation is non-convex due to multiplicative and ratio terms such as:
\[ \frac{W_{i+1}}{W_i}, \quad W_i l_i, \quad l_i^2 \]
To enable efficient optimization, we reformulate the problem using geometric programming techniques.

\paragraph{Logarithmic Variable Transformation}
We introduce the change of variables:
\[ x_i = \log W_i, \qquad y_i = \log l_i \]
which implies:
\[ W_i = e^{x_i}, \qquad l_i = e^{y_i} \]
This transformation is bijective over the positive orthant, ensuring no loss of generality.

\paragraph{Transformation of Nonlinear Terms}
Under this transformation, multiplicative and ratio terms become exponentials of affine expressions:
\[
	\frac{W_{i+1}}{W_i} = e^{x_{i+1}-x_i}, \quad
	W_i l_i = e^{x_i+y_i}, \quad
	l_i^2 = e^{2y_i}
\]
Thus, each term in the delay expressions can be written in the form:
\[ c_k e^{a_k^T z}, \quad \text{where } z = [x_1, \dots, x_N, y_1, \dots, y_N]^T \]

\paragraph{Convexity of the Log-Sum-Exp Function}
Define:
\[ f(x) = \log \left( \sum_{k=1}^{n} e^{x_k} \right) \]
Let $z_k = e^{x_k}$ and $z = [z_1, \dots, z_n]^T$. Then:
\[ \nabla^2 f(x) = \frac{1}{\mathbf{1}^T z} \mathrm{diag}(z) - \frac{1}{(\mathbf{1}^T z)^2} zz^T \]
For any $v \in \mathbb{R}^n$:
\[
	v^T \nabla^2 f(x) v
	= \frac{1}{(\mathbf{1}^T z)^2}
	\left[
		(\mathbf{1}^T z)\left(\sum_{i=1}^n z_i v_i^2\right)
		- \left(\sum_{i=1}^n z_i v_i\right)^2
		\right]
\]
By the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality:
\[ \left(\sum_{i=1}^n z_i v_i\right)^2 \le (\mathbf{1}^T z)\left(\sum_{i=1}^n z_i v_i^2\right) \]
Thus:
\[ v^T \nabla^2 f(x) v \ge 0 \quad \forall v \]
which proves that $f(x)$ is convex.

\paragraph{Convexity of the Delay Constraint}
The total delay can be written as:
\[ D(x,y) = \sum_k c_k e^{a_k^T z} \]
Taking logarithms:
\[ \log D(x,y) = \log \left( \sum_k e^{a_k^T z + \log c_k} \right) \]
which is a log-sum-exp function and therefore convex. Hence, the constraint:
\[ D(x,y) \le T_{clk} \]
is equivalent to the convex constraint:
\[ \log D(x,y) \le \log T_{clk} \]

\paragraph{Final Convex Optimization Problem}
The optimization problem becomes:
\begin{align}
	\min_{x,y} \quad & \log \left(
	C_{p0} e^{x_1} + \sum_{i=2}^{N} (C_{g0}+C_{p0}) e^{x_i}
	+ c \sum_{i=1}^{N} e^{y_i} \right)
\end{align}
\begin{align}
	\text{s.t.} \quad &
	\log \left( \sum_{i=1}^{N-1} d_i(x,y) + d_N(x,y) \right)
	\le \log T_{clk}
\end{align}
\[ \log W_{min} \le x_i \le \log W_{max}, \quad \forall i \]
\[ \log \epsilon \le y_i \le \log l_{max}, \quad \forall i \]

\paragraph{Strong Duality and Slater's Condition}
The problem is convex since:
\begin{itemize}
	\item The objective is a log-sum-exp function, hence convex.
	\item The delay constraint is convex.
	\item The bound constraints are affine.
\end{itemize}

Let the feasible set be:
\[
	\mathcal{D} =
	\left\{
	(x,y) \mid
	\log W_{min} \le x_i \le \log W_{max},\;
	\log \epsilon \le y_i \le \log l_{max}
	\right\}
\]

The relative interior of $\mathcal{D}$ is:
\[
	\mathrm{relint}(\mathcal{D}) =
	\left\{
	(x,y) \mid
	\log W_{min} < x_i < \log W_{max},\;
	\log \epsilon < y_i < \log l_{max}
	\right\}
\]

To verify Slater’s condition, we must show that there exists
$(x^\ast, y^\ast) \in \mathrm{relint}(\mathcal{D})$ such that:
\[
	\log \left( \sum_{i=1}^{N-1} d_i(x^\ast,y^\ast) + d_N(x^\ast,y^\ast) \right)
	< \log T_{clk}
\]

Such a point exists by choosing:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $y_i^\ast$ sufficiently small (close to $\log \epsilon$), which reduces all terms involving $e^{y_i}$,
	\item $x_i^\ast$ strictly within bounds and arranged to balance exponential terms.
\end{itemize}

Since all delay terms are positive and continuous, and can be made sufficiently small relative to $T_{clk}$, a strictly feasible point exists.

Therefore, Slater’s condition holds:
\[ \exists (x^\ast, y^\ast) \in \mathrm{relint}(\mathcal{D}) \]
such that \[  f_i(x^\ast,y^\ast) < 0 \]

\paragraph{Conclusion}
Since the problem is convex and Slater’s condition holds, strong duality follows:
\[
	p^\ast - d^\ast = 0
\]
Thus, the duality gap is zero, and the Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) conditions are necessary and sufficient for optimality.


\subsection{Dual Formulation}
Define
\begin{equation} \nonumber
	S_0(x,y)
	=
	C_{p0}e^{x_1}
	+\sum_{i=1}^N (C_{g0}+C_{p0})e^{x_i}
	+c\sum_{i=1}^N e^{y_i},
	\label{eq:S0}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation} \nonumber
	S_1(x,y)=\sum_{i=1}^{N-1} d_i(x,y)+d_N(x,y),
	\label{eq:S1}
\end{equation}
where, for \(i=1,\dots,N-1\)\\
The Lagrangian is
\begin{equation}
	\begin{aligned}
		\mathcal{L}(x,y,\lambda,\alpha,\beta,\gamma,\delta)
		={} &
		\log S_0(x,y)
		+\lambda\big(\log S_1(x,y)-\log T_{clk}\big) \\
		    & +\sum_{i=1}^N \alpha_i(\log W_{\min}-x_i)
		+\sum_{i=1}^N \beta_i(x_i-\log W_{\max})              \\
		    & +\sum_{i=1}^N \gamma_i(\log \varepsilon-y_i)
		+\sum_{i=1}^N \delta_i(y_i-\log l_{\max}).
	\end{aligned}
	\label{eq:Lagrangian}
\end{equation}
Using the conjugate of log-sum-exp
\begin{equation} \nonumber
	\log\!\left(\sum_j e^{s_j}\right)
	=
	\max_{\omega_j\ge 0,\ \sum_j \omega_j=1}
	\left(
	\sum_j \omega_j s_j-\sum_j \omega_j\log\omega_j
	\right),
	\label{eq:logsumexp1}
\end{equation}
introduce nonnegative weights \(\theta_0,\theta_i,\phi_i\) such that
\begin{equation} \nonumber
	\theta_0+\sum_{i=1}^N \theta_i+\sum_{i=1}^N \phi_i=1.
	\label{eq:theta-phi-sum}
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{equation}
	\begin{aligned}
		\log S_0(x,y)
		=
		\max_{\theta,\phi}\Bigg\{
		 & \theta_0(\log C_{p0}+x_1)
		+\sum_{i=1}^N \theta_i\big(\log(C_{g0}+C_{p0})+x_i\big) \\
		 & +\sum_{i=1}^N \phi_i(\log c+y_i)
		-\theta_0\log\theta_0
		-\sum_{i=1}^N \theta_i\log\theta_i
		-\sum_{i=1}^N \phi_i\log\phi_i
		\Bigg\}.
	\end{aligned}
	\label{eq:S0-dual}
\end{equation}
Using the scaled conjugate log-sum-exp
\begin{equation} \nonumber
	\lambda\log\!\left(\sum_j e^{s_j}\right)
	=
	\max_{\mu_j\ge 0,\ \sum_j\mu_j=\lambda}
	\left(
	\sum_j \mu_j s_j
	-\sum_j \mu_j\log\mu_j
	+\lambda\log\lambda
	\right),
	\label{eq:logsumexp2}
\end{equation}
introduce nonnegative variables \(\eta_{i,k}\), \(k=1,\dots,6\), such that
\begin{equation} \nonumber
	\sum_{i=1}^N\sum_{k=1}^6 \eta_{i,k}=\lambda.
	\label{eq:eta-sum}
\end{equation}
For \(i=1,\dots,N-1\), let
\begin{equation} \nonumber
	\eta_{i,1}\leftrightarrow R_0C_{g0}e^{x_{i+1}-x_i},\quad
	\eta_{i,2}\leftrightarrow R_0C_{p0},\quad
	\eta_{i,3}\leftrightarrow R_0ce^{y_i-x_i},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation} \nonumber
	\eta_{i,4}\leftrightarrow rC_{g0}e^{y_i+x_{i+1}},\quad
	\eta_{i,5}\leftrightarrow rC_{p0}e^{y_i+x_i},\quad
	\eta_{i,6}\leftrightarrow rce^{2y_i},
\end{equation}
and for \(i=N\),
\begin{equation} \nonumber
	\eta_{N,1}\leftrightarrow R_0C_Le^{-x_N},\quad
	\eta_{N,2}\leftrightarrow R_0C_{p0},\quad
	\eta_{N,3}\leftrightarrow R_0ce^{y_N-x_N},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation} \nonumber
	\eta_{N,4}\leftrightarrow rC_Le^{y_N},\quad
	\eta_{N,5}\leftrightarrow rC_{p0}e^{y_N+x_N},\quad
	\eta_{N,6}\leftrightarrow rce^{2y_N}.
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{equation}
	\begin{aligned}
		\lambda \log S_1(x,y)
		=
		\max_{\eta \ge 0,\ \sum \eta = \lambda}
		\Bigg\{
		 & \sum_{i=1}^{N-1}\sum_{k=1}^{6}
		\eta_{i,k}\bigl(\log a_{i,k}+m_{i,k}(x,y)\bigr)
		\\&+\sum_{k=1}^{6}
		\eta_{N,k}\bigl(\log a_{N,k}+m_{N,k}(x,y)\bigr)
		-\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{k=1}^{6}
		\eta_{i,k}\log \eta_{i,k}
		+\lambda \log \lambda
		\Bigg\}.
	\end{aligned}
	\label{eq:S1-dual}
\end{equation}
Substituting \eqref{eq:S0-dual} and \eqref{eq:S1-dual} into \eqref{eq:Lagrangian}, the resulting expression is affine in \(x\) and \(y\). Finite infimum over \(x\) and \(y\) requires the coefficients of each \(x_i\) and \(y_i\) to vanish.
From \(x_1\),
\begin{equation}\nonumber
	\theta_0+\theta_1-\eta_{1,1}-\eta_{1,3}+\eta_{1,5}-\alpha_1+\beta_1=0.
	\label{eq:x1-balance}
\end{equation}
From \(x_i\), \(i=2,\dots,N-1\),
\begin{equation}\nonumber
	\theta_i+\eta_{i-1,1}+\eta_{i-1,4}-\eta_{i,1}-\eta_{i,3}+\eta_{i,5}-\alpha_i+\beta_i=0.
	\label{eq:xi-balance}
\end{equation}
From \(x_N\),
\begin{equation}\nonumber
	\theta_N+\eta_{N-1,1}+\eta_{N-1,4}-\eta_{N,1}-\eta_{N,3}+\eta_{N,5}-\alpha_N+\beta_N=0.
	\label{eq:xN-balance}
\end{equation}
From \(y_i\), \(i=1,\dots,N\),
\begin{equation}\nonumber
	\phi_i+\eta_{i,3}+\eta_{i,4}+\eta_{i,5}+2\eta_{i,6}-\gamma_i+\delta_i=0.
	\label{eq:yi-balance}
\end{equation}
Then the dual function is
\begin{equation}\nonumber
	\begin{aligned}
		g
		=
		 & \;
		\theta_0\log\frac{C_{p0}}{\theta_0}
		+\sum_{i=1}^N \theta_i\log\frac{C_{g0}+C_{p0}}{\theta_i}
		+\sum_{i=1}^N \phi_i\log\frac{c}{\phi_i}
		\\
		 & +\sum_{i=1}^{N-1}\Bigg[
			\eta_{i,1}\log\frac{R_0C_{g0}}{\eta_{i,1}}
			+\eta_{i,2}\log\frac{R_0C_{p0}}{\eta_{i,2}}
			+\eta_{i,3}\log\frac{R_0c}{\eta_{i,3}}
			+\eta_{i,4}\log\frac{rC_{g0}}{\eta_{i,4}}
			+\eta_{i,5}\log\frac{rC_{p0}}{\eta_{i,5}}
			+\eta_{i,6}\log\frac{rc}{\eta_{i,6}}
			\Bigg]
		\\
		 & +\eta_{N,1}\log\frac{R_0C_L}{\eta_{N,1}}
		+\eta_{N,2}\log\frac{R_0C_{p0}}{\eta_{N,2}}
		+\eta_{N,3}\log\frac{R_0c}{\eta_{N,3}}
		+\eta_{N,4}\log\frac{rC_L}{\eta_{N,4}}
		+\eta_{N,5}\log\frac{rC_{p0}}{\eta_{N,5}}
		+\eta_{N,6}\log\frac{rc}{\eta_{N,6}}
		\\
		 & +\lambda\log\lambda-\lambda\log T_{clk}
		+\sum_{i=1}^N \alpha_i\log W_{\min}
		-\sum_{i=1}^N \beta_i\log W_{\max}
		+\sum_{i=1}^N \gamma_i\log\varepsilon
		-\sum_{i=1}^N \delta_i\log l_{\max}.
	\end{aligned}
	\label{eq:dual-function}
\end{equation}
Therefore, the dual problem is
\begin{equation}\nonumber
	\begin{aligned}
		\max_{\theta,\phi,\eta,\lambda,\alpha,\beta,\gamma,\delta}\quad & 
		g\\
		\text{s.t.}\quad
		    & \theta_0\ge 0,\qquad \theta_i\ge 0,\qquad \phi_i\ge 0,\qquad i=1,\dots,N,\\
		    & \eta_{i,k}\ge 0,\qquad i=1,\dots,N,\quad k=1,\dots,6,\\
		    & \alpha_i,\beta_i,\gamma_i,\delta_i\ge 0,\qquad i=1,\dots,N,\\
		    & \theta_0+\sum_{i=1}^N \theta_i+\sum_{i=1}^N \phi_i=1,\\
		    & \sum_{i=1}^N\sum_{k=1}^6 \eta_{i,k}=\lambda,\\
		    & \theta_0+\theta_1-\eta_{1,1}-\eta_{1,3}+\eta_{1,5}-\alpha_1+\beta_1=0,\\
		    & \theta_i+\eta_{i-1,1}+\eta_{i-1,4}-\eta_{i,1}-\eta_{i,3}+\eta_{i,5}-\alpha_i+\beta_i=0,\quad i=2,\dots,N-1,\\
		    & \theta_N+\eta_{N-1,1}+\eta_{N-1,4}-\eta_{N,1}-\eta_{N,3}+\eta_{N,5}-\alpha_N+\beta_N=0,\\
		    & \phi_i+\eta_{i,3}+\eta_{i,4}+\eta_{i,5}+2\eta_{i,6}-\gamma_i+\delta_i=0,\quad i=1,\dots,N.
	\end{aligned}
	\label{eq:dual-problem}
\end{equation}
\subsection{KKT Conditions}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Primal feasibility:
		\begin{equation}\nonumber
			\begin{aligned}
				&\log S_1(x,y) \leq \log T_{clk}\\
				&\log W_{\min}\leq x_i \leq \log W_{\max},\forall i\\
				&\log \epsilon \leq y_i \leq \log l_{\max},\forall i
			\end{aligned}
		\end{equation}
	\item Dual feasibility:
		\[
		\lambda \geq 0, \quad \alpha_i \geq 0, \quad \beta_i \geq 0, \quad \gamma_i \geq 0, \quad \delta_i \geq 0, \quad \forall i
		\]
	\item Complementary slackness:
		\begin{equation}\nonumber
			\begin{aligned}
				&\lambda(\log S_1(x,y) - \log T_{clk}) = 0\\
				&\alpha_i(\log W_{\min} - x_i) = 0, \forall i\\
				&\beta_i(x_i - \log W_{\max}) = 0, \forall i\\
				&\gamma_i(\log \epsilon - y_i) = 0, \forall i\\
				&\delta_i(y_i - \log l_{\max}) = 0, \forall i
			\end{aligned}
		\end{equation}
	\item Stationarity:
		\begin{equation}\nonumber
			\begin{aligned}
				\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_i} &= \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i} \log S_0(x,y) + \lambda \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i} (\log S_1(x,y) - \log T_{clk}) - \alpha_i + \beta_i\\
				&=\frac{C_{p0}e^{x_1}+(C_{g0}+C_{p0})e^{x_i}}{S_0(x,y)}\\
				&+\frac{\lambda(R_0C_{g0}e^{x_i-x_{i-1}}+rC_{go}e^{y_{i-1}+x_i}-R_0C_{g0}e^{x_{i+1}-x_i}-R_0cE^{y_i-x_i}+rC_{p0}e^{y_i+x_i})}{S_1(x,y)}-\alpha_i+\beta_i=0
			\end{aligned}
		\end{equation}
		\begin{equation}
			\begin{aligned}\nonumber
				\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial y_i} &= \frac{\partial}{\partial y_i} \log S_0(x,y) + \lambda \frac{\partial}{\partial y_i} (\log S_1(x,y) - \log T_{clk}) - \gamma_i + \delta_i\\
				&=\frac{ce^{y_i}}{S_0(x,y)}+\frac{\lambda(R_0ce^{y_i-x_i}+rC_{g0}e^{y_i+x_{i+1}}+rC_{p0}e^{y_i+x_i}+2rce^{2y_i})}{S_1(x,y)}-\gamma_i+\delta_i=0
			\end{aligned}
		\end{equation}
\end{itemize}

\section{Approach}

\subsection{Simulation Strategy and Design Choices}

Although the theoretical formulation developed in the previous sections treats both inverter widths $\{W_i\}$ and wire lengths $\{l_i\}$ as optimization variables, a direct implementation of this fully unconstrained problem leads to degenerate solutions in practice. In particular, since both capacitance and delay decrease with smaller wire lengths, the optimizer tends to drive $l_i$ to their minimum allowable values, resulting in physically uninformative solutions.

To address this issue and obtain meaningful design insights, we adopt a two-stage experimental strategy:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Fixed-profile wire simulations:} We fix the wire lengths $\{l_i\}$ according to predefined profiles and optimize only the inverter widths $\{W_i\}$. This allows us to isolate the effect of wire distribution on optimal sizing while avoiding degeneracy.
    
    \item \textbf{Unrestricted optimization:} We subsequently solve the full problem where both $\{W_i\}$ and $\{l_i\}$ are variables. This experiment is used primarily to validate the theoretical formulation and to study primal-dual consistency.
\end{enumerate}

This separation enables both interpretability and theoretical validation within the same framework.

\subsection{Parameter Selection and Operating Regime}

To ensure that the optimization produces nontrivial and informative solutions, we carefully selected a parameter regime that avoids boundary-dominated behavior. In particular, we chose:

\[ N = 6, \quad \sum_{i=1}^N l_i = 25, \quad T_{clk} = 90, \quad C_L = 15 \]

This regime was empirically tuned to ensure that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The delay constraint is active but not overly restrictive
    \item The optimal widths are not trivially pinned at $W_{min}$
    \item Wire effects meaningfully influence the solution
\end{itemize}

This allows us to observe the interaction between sizing, interconnect distribution, and timing constraints.

\subsection{Wire Length Profiles}

In the fixed-profile setting, we define several representative wire distributions to study how interconnect placement affects optimal design:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Uniform profile:} Wire length is evenly distributed across all stages
    \item \textbf{Back-loaded profile:} Larger wire segments appear near the output stages
    \item \textbf{Single-hotspot profile:} One stage is assigned a significantly larger wire segment than the others
\end{itemize}

These profiles are designed to reflect different physical layout scenarios and allow us to study how downstream loading and localized interconnect concentration influence optimal inverter sizing.
\subsection{Optimization Methodology}

The optimization problem is implemented using the geometric programming formulation derived earlier. Specifically:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Decision variables are transformed into the log-domain:
    \[ x_i = \log W_i, \quad y_i = \log l_i \]
    
    \item The delay constraint is expressed as a log-sum-exp function, ensuring convexity
    
    \item The objective function remains a sum of exponentials, which is compatible with convex solvers
\end{itemize}

The resulting convex problem is solved using \textbf{CVXPY}, which provides a high-level interface for specifying and solving convex optimization problems. This allows us to efficiently compute globally optimal solutions for each experimental configuration.

\subsection{Baseline Comparison}

To evaluate the importance of modeling interconnect effects, we compare the full wire-aware model with a simplified baseline inspired by classical formulations (e.g., logical effort or gate-only models) \cite{baseline}. In this baseline:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Wire resistance and capacitance terms are removed
    \item Only gate and parasitic capacitances are considered
\end{itemize}

This comparison highlights the extent to which neglecting interconnect effects underestimates the cost of meeting timing constraints.

\subsection{Unrestricted Formulation and Dual Validation}

In addition to the fixed-profile experiments, we also solve the unrestricted formulation where both $\{W_i\}$ and $\{l_i\}$ are optimized simultaneously. While this formulation is less physically informative due to degeneracy, it serves two important purposes:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Verification of theoretical results:} We compare the primal and dual objective values to confirm strong duality.
    \item \textbf{Numerical consistency:} We evaluate the duality gap in the log-domain to ensure correctness of the convex reformulation.
\end{itemize}

This step provides additional confidence in the validity of the geometric programming approach.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

Across all experiments, we evaluate:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Optimal objective value:} Represents the minimized capacitance (and thus dynamic power)
    \item \textbf{Timing ratio:} Ratio of achieved delay to $T_{clk}$, indicating constraint tightness
    \item \textbf{Width allocation:} Stage-wise inverter sizing patterns
    \item \textbf{Sensitivity to parameters:} Changes in objective under varying $T_{clk}$ and wire profiles
\end{itemize}

These metrics allow us to systematically analyze both quantitative performance and qualitative design behavior.


\section{Results And Conclusion}

\subsection{Results}

Although the theoretical development in the previous sections treats both inverter widths and wire lengths as optimization variables, our simulation study begins with a fixed-profile setting for the wire lengths. This choice avoids the potentially degenerate behavior of the unrestricted formulation and yields more interpretable design trade-offs under wire-aware timing constraints. After presenting these main experiments, we return to the unrestricted formulation to align the numerical study with the earlier primal-dual analysis.

We first selected a feasible and informative operating regime so that the optimizer would not trivially pin all gate widths at their lower bounds. The selected setting was $N=6$, total wire length $=25$, $T_{clk}=90$, and $C_L=15$. Under this tuned regime, the fixed-profile simulation produced nontrivial width allocations, which allowed us to observe how timing pressure and wire distribution affect the optimal design.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.78\linewidth]{simulation/plots/power_vs_Tclk.png}
    \caption{Optimal objective versus timing budget for the fixed-profile wire-aware model under three wire profiles.}
    \label{fig:power_tclk}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:power_tclk} shows the timing-budget sweep for the fixed-profile model. As the clock period $T_{clk}$ becomes tighter, the optimal objective increases across all tested wire profiles. This trend is consistent with the model structure: a stricter Elmore delay constraint forces more aggressive sizing, which raises the capacitance-related power objective. Among the tested profiles, the back-loaded interconnect profile is generally the most expensive, reflecting the stronger downstream delay penalty created by longer wires near the output end of the chain.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.78\linewidth]{simulation/plots/width_allocation_profiles.png}
    \caption{Stage-by-stage optimal width allocation under three fixed wire profiles in the tuned operating regime.}
    \label{fig:width_profiles}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:width_profiles} shows the stage-by-stage width allocation for the three wire profiles in the representative operating regime. In the back-loaded case, the optimized objective was approximately $1.4414$, with a timing ratio of $1.00000049$, and the resulting widths were
\[
[0.9673,\ 0.5243,\ 0.5000,\ 0.5084,\ 0.6338,\ 1.4383].
\]
These values are clearly nonuniform, and the larger widths near the output side, together with the uneven allocation across stages, indicate that the optimizer compensates for heavier downstream loading and larger wire-delay contributions. This provides a direct qualitative explanation for why a wire-aware model can produce different sizing recommendations than a gate-only approximation.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.90\linewidth]{simulation/plots/full_vs_baseline.png}
    \caption{Objective comparison between the full wire-aware model and a Boyd-style simplified baseline across timing budgets.}
    \label{fig:baseline_compare}
\end{figure}

To benchmark the wire-aware model, we compared it with a Boyd-style simplified baseline that removes wire resistance and wire-capacitance coupling terms. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:baseline_compare}, the simplified baseline yields a substantially lower objective value than the full model under matched settings. In our tested timing range, the baseline stays near approximately $1.024$, whereas the full wire-aware model ranges from about $1.303$ to $1.355$. This does not mean that the simplified formulation is better in a physical sense; rather, it is more optimistic because it neglects the interconnect delay terms that are explicitly modeled in the full GP. Therefore, the comparison suggests that approaches that do not include wire-delay effects can significantly underestimate the power-related cost required to satisfy a timing target.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.90\linewidth]{simulation/plots/free_length_profiles.png}
    \caption{Optimal widths and wire lengths in the unrestricted formulation where both $W_i$ and $l_i$ are optimized.}
    \label{fig:free_length}
\end{figure}

Finally, we returned to the unrestricted formulation in which both $W_i$ and $l_i$ are optimized. Figure~\ref{fig:free_length} shows that, in the tested regime, the unrestricted primal drives both the inverter widths and the wire lengths to their lower bounds, up to very small numerical deviations. This confirms the degeneracy concern that motivated the fixed-profile setup in the main simulation. At the same time, the unrestricted primal objective was $1.0576163$, the dual-implied objective was $1.0576159$, and the log-domain duality gap was approximately $3.94 \times 10^{-7}$. This provides numerical support for the dual formulation while also showing that the unrestricted problem, although mathematically consistent, is less physically informative for simulation purposes.
